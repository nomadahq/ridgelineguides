California LLC Statement of Information: Deadline, Cost, and Filing Guide (2026)

Last updated: February 2026 · Applies to: California LLCs

California LLCs are required to file a Statement of Information (Form LLC-12) with the California Secretary of State every two years. Additionally, most California LLCs must pay an annual $800 franchise tax to the Franchise Tax Board. These are two separate requirements — this guide covers both.

Statement of Information (Form LLC-12)

The Statement of Information is a biennial filing that updates the California Secretary of State on your LLC's basic information. It confirms or updates your LLC's:

  • Business address(es)
  • Agent for service of process (California's term for registered agent)
  • Manager or member names (depending on management structure)
  • Type of business

When is it due?

Your initial Statement of Information is due within 90 days of forming your LLC. After that, it is due every two years during the applicable filing period. Your filing period is based on the month your LLC was formed — the Secretary of State will send a reminder.

Filing steps

The $800 California franchise tax

In addition to the Statement of Information, most California LLCs must pay an annual franchise tax of $800 to the Franchise Tax Board (FTB). This is a flat fee that applies regardless of your LLC's income — even if your LLC earned nothing.

The $800 franchise tax is separate from the Statement of Information. Many new LLC owners confuse these two requirements. The $20 Statement of Information goes to the Secretary of State. The $800 franchise tax goes to the Franchise Tax Board. Both must be paid to keep your LLC in good standing.

First-year exemption

California enacted legislation that exempts LLCs from the $800 franchise tax in their first taxable year, provided the LLC was formed on or after January 1, 2021. Beginning in your second taxable year, the $800 annual franchise tax applies.

Gross receipts fee

In addition to the $800 franchise tax, California LLCs with gross receipts above $250,000 must pay an additional fee based on total income:

Total California incomeAdditional fee
$250,000 – $499,999$900
$500,000 – $999,999$2,500
$1,000,000 – $4,999,999$6,000
$5,000,000 or more$11,790

What happens if you don't file?

If you fail to file the Statement of Information, the California Secretary of State may suspend or cancel your LLC. If you fail to pay the franchise tax, the Franchise Tax Board will impose penalties and interest, and may also suspend your LLC.

A suspended LLC cannot legally conduct business in California, cannot file lawsuits, and loses access to California courts to defend itself.

Do you need a registered agent in California?

California uses the term "agent for service of process" rather than registered agent. Every California LLC must designate an agent for service of process with a physical California street address. This agent receives legal documents on your behalf.

Frequently asked questions

Can I avoid the $800 franchise tax?

Not if you want to maintain an active LLC in California. The only exemption is for the first taxable year (for LLCs formed after January 1, 2021). After that, the $800 is due annually regardless of income. Some business owners choose to form their LLC in another state to avoid this tax, but if you are doing business in California, you will still need to register as a foreign LLC and pay the tax.

What is the difference between the Statement of Information and the franchise tax?

The Statement of Information ($20, biennial) is an informational filing with the Secretary of State that updates your LLC's records. The franchise tax ($800, annual) is a tax payment to the Franchise Tax Board for the privilege of doing business in California. Both are required.

How do I check my LLC's status?

Search for your LLC on bizfile Online to see its current status, filing history, and whether any filings are due.
