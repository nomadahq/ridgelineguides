Software and Subscription Deductions for Business (2026)

Last updated: February 2026 · Applies to: Self-employed individuals, freelancers, small business owners

If you pay for software, apps, or online subscriptions to run your business, those costs are generally deductible as ordinary business expenses. This includes everything from accounting software and project management tools to cloud storage, design apps, and industry-specific platforms. With most businesses now running on SaaS subscriptions, this deduction can add up to thousands of dollars per year.

Quick summary

Business software and subscriptions are deductible as ordinary business expenses on Schedule C. This includes SaaS tools, cloud services, apps, and online platforms used for business. If a tool is used for both business and personal purposes, only the business-use portion is deductible.

How the deduction works

Software subscriptions and online tools are deducted as ordinary and necessary business expenses under IRS Section 162. Monthly or annual SaaS subscriptions are fully deductible in the year they are paid. If you purchase perpetual software (a one-time license), you may need to depreciate it over 3 years or deduct it immediately under Section 179, depending on the cost and your situation.

What qualifies

  • Accounting software (QuickBooks, Xero, FreshBooks, Wave)
  • Project management tools (Asana, Monday.com, Trello, ClickUp)
  • Communication tools (Slack, Zoom, Microsoft Teams, Google Workspace)
  • Design and creative tools (Adobe Creative Cloud, Canva, Figma)
  • Email marketing platforms (Mailchimp, ConvertKit, Constant Contact)
  • Cloud storage (Dropbox, Google Drive, iCloud for business)
  • Website hosting and domain registration
  • CRM software (HubSpot, Salesforce, Pipedrive)
  • Industry-specific software (legal, medical, construction, etc.)
  • Antivirus and security software used for business

What does not qualify

  • Personal subscriptions (Netflix, Spotify, personal streaming)
  • Software used entirely for personal purposes
  • The personal-use portion of mixed-use software
  • Video game subscriptions (unless you are in the gaming industry)

How to calculate the deduction

Add up all business software and subscription costs paid during the year. If a subscription is used for both business and personal purposes, estimate the business-use percentage and deduct only that portion. For example, if you pay $15/month for a cloud storage plan used 80% for business, your annual deduction is $15 x 12 x 80% = $144. For fully business-use subscriptions, deduct 100% of the cost.

Documentation you need

Keep these records

Receipts or billing statements for each subscription
A list of all business software and subscriptions with annual costs
Notes on business purpose for each tool (in case of audit)
Business-use percentage estimates for mixed-use tools

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Forgetting to deduct small subscriptions that add up ($10/month tools)
  • Not keeping receipts for annual subscriptions paid by credit card
  • Deducting 100% of a mixed-use subscription without documenting business-use percentage
  • Missing the deduction for website hosting and domain costs
  • Not categorizing software expenses consistently on Schedule C
IRS audit note: Software deductions are generally low-risk for audits because they are clearly business expenses. The main risk is claiming personal subscriptions as business expenses. Keep a list of all subscriptions with business justification.

Frequently asked questions

Where do I report software subscriptions on my tax return?

On Schedule C, software subscriptions are typically reported under "Other Expenses" (Line 27a) or "Office Expenses" (Line 18). Be consistent in your categorization from year to year.

Can I deduct a new computer or phone?

Yes, but hardware is a separate deduction. Computers and phones are typically deducted under Section 179 or depreciated. Software subscriptions are separate operating expenses.

What if I pay annually instead of monthly?

Annual payments are deductible in the year paid. There is no difference in deductibility between monthly and annual billing.
